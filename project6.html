Teaching and Museum-Based Education

Although research can often become the singular focus in academia, I believe teaching plays an equal, if not greater, role in the success of the university community. However well a research project has been designed and implemented, it depends on communicating methods, assumptions, and findings to be effectiveâ€”teaching is a regular test of these skills as a researcher. With this perspective, I have developed a teaching philosophy that emphasizes hands-on experiences, applied material, and clear, measurable outcomes. I challenge students to consider how topics in archaeology can transcend archaeological applications, but how they might fit into wider discussions of sustainability and global citizenship.

I have classroom experience teaching introductory and methods-based courses, including Introduction to Archaeology, Human Impacts on Ancient Environments, and Independent Studies in Paleoecological Methods. I have also taught in non-traditional settings, such as museum-based educational programs and field schools at the University of Oregon and as an instructor in the Osher Lifelong Learning Institute at Arizona State University.
